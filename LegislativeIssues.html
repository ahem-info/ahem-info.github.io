AHEM’s position on federal homeschooling legislation AHEM’s philosophy is to strengthen the grassroots presence of homeschoolers, as our voices are strongest at the local and state level. It follows that it is wise to keep home-schooling out of federal law as much as possible to avoid further regulation and to keep the local, grassroots strength of homeschooling alive.

Bills raising the mandatory attendance age
The Education committee has held hearings on many of the bills concerning compulsory attendance age summarized below, but has reported only one favorably out of committee, S 251.

S251
AN ACT RELATIVE TO DROPOUT PREVENTION AND RECOVERY 
Sonia Chang-Diaz 
July 23, 2015 — Bill reported favorably by committee and referred to the committee on Senate Ways and Means
May 12, 2015 — Hearing
April 15, 2015 — Referred to Joint Committee on Education

SECTION 2. Section 1B of chapter 69 of the General Laws, as appearing in the 2012 Official Edition, is hereby amended by inserting after the word “attendance”, in line 114, the following words:- ; provided, however, that all children under the age of 18 shall be required to attend school if they have not graduated from high school.
Summary: In addition to implementing various measures to reduce dropping out of high school, this bill raises the compulsory attendance age from 16 to 18.
AHEM position: Opposed. AHEM submitted a letter to be included with the official hearing testimony, opposing the bill because raising the compulsory attendance age would not result in a higher rate of high school completion, so the financial burden to taxpayers and already fiscally strapped school departments cannot be justified. AHEM is also concerned that raising the age to 18 could potentially remove alternatives, such as early graduation, from motivated students for whom traditional public school is not working.

Bill concerning homeschoolers and public libraries

H 427
AN ACT RELATIVE TO MASSACHUSETTS PUBLIC LIBRARIES AND HOME EDUCATED CHILDREN
Kevin J. Kuros
Sept. 9, 2015 — Hearing
Mar 10, 2015 — Referred to the committee on Education
See the full text of H427 here : https://malegislature.gov/Bills/189/House/H427
Summary: This bill was filed so that home educated students who live in towns in MA whose libraries have been decertified by the state due to not meeting the minimum requirements for funding, staffing and hours will have the ability to borrow books from other towns’ libraries.

Background: When a library is decertified, it loses its right to cooperative borrowing privileges with other libraries. While local libraries are free to make their own policies regarding whether or not to allow residents of a decertified town to have borrowing privileges, the recommendation from the Massachusetts Board of Library Commissioners is to not extend borrowing privileges to decertified town residents in order to impress on decertified towns the importance of supporting their local library, and to give those in town a good argument in fighting for funding for the library. Due to this policy, the number of decertified libraries in MA is extremely small.
AHEM’s position: Opposed. We do not think homeschooled students should be singled out for special privileges over anyone else in the state; we do not think the state should legislate what is now a municipal funding and policy decision; and we stand strong with libraries in supporting their policy which has been successful in maintaining a strong library system across the state. Ultimately we feel that there most likely exists a more effective way to get books into the hands of homeschooled students in decertified towns that would not impact all homeschoolers and libraries across the state.

AN ACT RELATIVE TO MASSACHUSETTS PUBLIC LIBRARIES AND HOME EDUCATED CHILDREN
Kevin J. Kuros
Sept. 9, 2015 — Hearing Mar 10, 2015 — Referred to the committee on Education SECTION 1. Section 8 of Chapter 78 of the General Laws, as appearing in the 2012 Official Edition, is hereby amended by inserting after the first paragraph, the following sentence:

Ultimately we feel that there most likely exists a more effective way to get books into the hands of homeschooled students in decertified towns that would not impact all homeschoolers and libraries across the state.

On top of all that, H 427 introduces the term “home educated” into statutory language, begging that it be defined, and possibly creating more problems for homeschoolers than it solves. Bill concerning homeschoolers and public libraries

H 427
AN ACT RELATIVE TO MASSACHUSETTS PUBLIC LIBRARIES AND HOME EDUCATED CHILDREN
Kevin J. Kuros
Jan 20, 2015 — Referred to the committee on Education
SECTION 1. Section 8 of Chapter 78 of the General Laws, as appearing in the 2012 Official Edition, is hereby amended by inserting after the first paragraph, the following sentence:

No free town public library shall refuse to loan its books or other library material to any home educated child residing in the Commonwealth of Massachusetts, provided that proof of home education is presented upon the loan request.

Summary: This bill was filed so that home educated students who live in towns in MA whose libraries have been decertified by the state due to not meeting the minimum requirements for funding, staffing and hours will have the ability to borrow books from other towns’ libraries.

Background: When a library is decertified, it loses its right to cooperative borrowing privileges with other libraries. While local libraries are free to make their own policies regarding whether or not to allow residents of a decertified town to have borrowing privileges, the recommendation from the Massachusetts Board of Library Commissioners is to not extend borrowing privileges to decertified town residents in order to impress on decertified towns the importance of supporting their local library, and to give those in town a good argument in fighting for funding for the library. Due to this policy, the number of decertified libraries in MA is extremely small.

AHEM’s position: Opposed. We do not think homeschooled students should be singled out for special privileges over anyone else in the state; we do not think the state should legislate what is now a municipal funding and policy decision; and we stand with libraries in supporting their policy which has been successful in maintaining a strong library system across the state. Ultimately we feel that there most likely exists a more effective way to get books into the hands of homeschooled students in decertified towns that would not impact all homeschoolers and libraries across the state.

On top of all that, H 427 introduces the term “home educated” into statutory law, begging that it be defined, and possibly creating more problems for homeschoolers than it solves. We have discussed our concerns with Rep. Kuros. While Rep. Kuros still feels his bill is necessary, he has agreed to adopt an amendment to his bill to use “otherwise instructed” instead of “home educated.”
